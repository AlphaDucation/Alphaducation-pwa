Spaced Repetition: a research toolbox for tracing what lasts

A rigorous, research-facing support file for graduate students, advanced educators, and researchers working on spaced practice, retrieval practice, and the difference between lesson performance and durable mathematical learning.

WP-008 Series: The Weekly Proof Complexity: Discover Topic: Spaced Practice Themes: Memory · Retrieval Practice · Spacing Effect
Research orientation

Start with the strongest claim, then study the edges.

The most stable claim in the provided research is that distributed practice improves delayed retention better than massed practice in general learning, and that the effect also appears in mathematics-specific evidence.

What remains unsettled is not whether spacing matters, but how it should be designed in mathematics without shrinking the subject into fragments that are easy to rehearse and hard to understand.

2 · Core concepts map

What belongs to the center of this topic

Use this map to keep the conceptual field stable while reading. The central idea is not an app, but a timing structure for learning.

Spacing effect

The empirical pattern that learning spread over time is retained better than learning compressed into one sitting. Cepeda et al. (2006) provide one of the major quantitative syntheses.

Distributed practice

A design principle for arranging study or practice episodes across time. It is the broader instructional form in which the spacing effect becomes visible.

Retrieval practice

The act of recalling previously learned information from memory rather than merely rereading it. Agarwal, Nunes, and Blunt (2021) treat it as a learning process, not only an assessment event.

Testing effect

The finding that retrieval can produce better later retention than restudy. In mathematics, Murray, Horner, and Göbel (2025) caution that this specific comparison is not yet robust.

Interleaving

Mixing problem types so students must identify the right method. Rohrer and Taylor (2007) show that mixed mathematics practice can improve later learning.

Massed practice

Compressed practice on one type of task or one body of content. It often creates stronger immediate performance than longer-term retention.

Who structures the conversation

Scholar / tradition Role in this topic Why they matter here
Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted, & Rohrer Foundational learning-sciences synthesis Cepeda et al. (2006) anchor the broad claim that distributed practice improves delayed recall better than massing across a large body of studies.
Pashler et al. Translation from cognitive science to instructional guidance The 2007 Institute of Education Sciences practice guide turns the spacing literature into concrete recommendations for planning instruction.
Dunlosky et al. Technique-comparison literature Their 2013 review places distributed practice and practice testing among the highest-utility learning techniques, useful for locating spacing among competing study strategies.
Rohrer & Taylor Mathematics-specific design work Their work on shuffled problem sets matters because it shows that mathematics practice structure changes what students are learning to notice and retrieve.
Lyle, Bego, Hopkins, Hieb, Ralston, Immekus Mathematics-specific retrieval and spacing studies These authors move the conversation from general memory experiments into precalculus and calculus settings, where cumulative dependency is educationally consequential.
Murray, Horner, & Göbel Current mathematics meta-analytic anchor The 2025 synthesis is the best mathematics-specific overview in the provided research: it supports spacing while resisting overclaiming about retrieval versus restudy.

4 · Source-type guide

What kind of source should answer what kind of question?

Foundational syntheses

Use these when you need the strongest claims and broadest convergence: Cepeda et al. (2006), Dunlosky et al. (2013), and Murray et al. (2025).

Best for: defining settled claims Risk: overgeneralising to every context

Practice guides and applied reviews

Use these when you want to connect theory to planning and classroom routines. Pashler et al. (2007) and Agarwal et al. (2021) are the most useful anchors here.

Best for: design translation Risk: institutional smoothing of nuance

Mathematics-specific intervention studies

Use these when your question is discipline-sensitive: precalculus, calculus, mixed problem sets, durable course knowledge, and delayed retention in mathematics.

Best for: subject specificity Risk: narrower samples

Regional and francophone lenses

Use these when you want to avoid reading the topic only through anglophone cognitive efficiency. They widen the frame toward scenario design, motivation, milieu, and didactic tradition.

Best for: theoretical reframing Risk: not always direct evidence on spacing
5 · Concept comparison grid

Keep related terms close, but not interchangeable

Concept What it means What it is often confused with Research note
Spaced practice Practice episodes distributed across time. Interleaving, repetition in general, “using Anki.” Strongest overall claim in the dossier; mathematics-specific support is real, though not dramatic.
Retrieval practice Actively recalling information from memory. Any quiz, any revision, any test. Broadly supported across classrooms, but mathematics-specific comparison with restudy is less settled.
Testing effect The gain from retrieval over restudy. Retrieval practice itself. Useful distinction: activity versus observed effect.
Interleaving Mixing problem types to force method selection. Spacing as a whole. Interleaving often creates spacing, but it adds a discrimination demand that spacing alone does not.
Massed practice Practising one type or topic in a concentrated block. Efficient teaching. Often produces stronger immediate fluency, which can mislead teachers and learners about what was learned.
6 · Claim-evidence tracker

Track what is stable, debated, emerging, or thin

Claim Status Best supporting source(s) Main caveat
Distributed practice improves delayed retention better than massed practice in general learning. Settled Cepeda et al. (2006) This is a broad claim across tasks; it still needs interpretation when moved into mathematics-specific contexts.
Spacing helps mathematics retention. Settled Lyle et al. (2020); Murray et al. (2025) The effect is real, but smaller and more qualified than popular claims often imply.
Retrieval practice is broadly useful in schools and classrooms. Settled Agarwal et al. (2021) Representativeness is limited; only a small share of studies came from non-WEIRD contexts.
Testing outperforms restudy robustly in mathematics. Contested Agarwal et al. (2021); Murray et al. (2025) The general retrieval literature is stronger than the mathematics-specific comparison.
There is one universally optimal spacing schedule. Contested Cepeda et al. (2008, cited in dossier) The best interval depends on the retention horizon and context.
Spacing produces short-term difficulty that may support long-term retention. Emerging Lyle et al. (2022) Promising in calculus; broader STEM evidence is still mixed in the dossier.
Anki itself is the key evidence-based solution for mathematics learning. Thin Anki documentation; spacing literature indirectly The evidence is stronger for the principles than for the specific product.
Spacing alone improves proof-writing, modeling, or far transfer in mathematics. Thin Dossier caution note The evidence is much stronger for retention of learned content than for sophisticated mathematical production.
7 · Debate / tension map

Where the literature resists simplification

Performance now vs learning later

Lyle et al. (2022) matter because they show that a better long-term design can look worse during practice. This tension is central for mathematics, where teachers often judge success from immediate fluency.

General memory science vs mathematics specificity

The broad literature strongly supports spacing and often supports retrieval, but Murray et al. (2025) caution that some general claims do not transfer cleanly into mathematics when the comparison is narrowed.

Cognitive efficiency vs didactic design

Anglophone studies often ask whether a timing pattern improves retention. The francophone didactic lens asks how mathematical knowledge, classroom situations, and scenario design shape what learning is allowed to become.

Tool enthusiasm vs tool realism

A planner or flashcard system can organise revisits, but it cannot substitute for concept formation, explanation, proof, or transfer. A tool helps only if the underlying knowledge has been meaningfully built.

9 · Literature review supports

Tools for reading, annotating, and writing

Source evaluation checklist foundational or secondary?
Research-gap prompts where to push next
  • What mathematics-specific outcomes remain under-studied beyond retention of procedures and facts?
  • Where does the literature still treat mathematics as if it were just another memory domain?
  • What happens to proof, transfer, and modeling when spacing is foregrounded?
  • Which contexts, languages, and educational systems are missing from the evidence base?
  • What would a didactically richer design of spacing look like?
Quotation / evidence organiser from note to draft
Methodological reflection prompts read beyond the finding
  • What kind of learning outcome is being measured here, and what kind is left invisible?
  • Does the design privilege recall of discrete knowledge over richer mathematical activity?
  • What is the retention interval, and why does that interval matter?
  • What does this design assume about instructional time, curriculum structure, or institutional stability?
  • Would the same claim look different in a proof-heavy, inquiry-based, or non-WEIRD context?
Regional lens prompts do not read only through one tradition
  • How would this claim sound if rewritten through didactic engineering rather than cognitive efficiency?
  • What is gained by Riopel and McMullin’s attention to school implementation and learner motivation?
  • How do Musial, Pradère, and Tricot shift the discussion from technique effectiveness to pedagogical scenario design?
  • What does the French didactic tradition make visible about mathematical knowledge that the mainstream spacing literature can leave unnamed?
Mini literature review scaffold usable writing sequence
  1. Open with the strongest settled claim: distributed practice improves delayed retention better than massing.
  2. Narrow to mathematics: show that mathematics-specific evidence supports spacing, but with more modest and qualified claims.
  3. Separate spacing from retrieval and testing-effect claims.
  4. Introduce the mathematics-specific caveat: retrieval versus restudy is not yet robust in the current meta-analysis.
  5. Add the regional / francophone divergence: scenario design, didactic framing, and the nature of mathematical knowledge matter.
  6. Close with the unresolved question rather than a slogan.
10 + 11 · Research questions / future directions

What should a strong next question sound like?

Open problem

Mathematics beyond retention

How can mathematics instruction use spaced and retrieval-rich designs without reducing the subject to items that are easy to rehearse and hard to think with?

Open problem

Regional expansion

How do spacing and retrieval claims look in non-WEIRD contexts, and what is altered when local curricular structures or pedagogical traditions differ from the populations most studied?

Open problem

Proof and transfer

What forms of spaced practice support proof-writing, modeling, and conceptual reorganisation rather than only durable access to previously learned items?

Candidate research-question stems

  • In mathematics classrooms, when does spaced retrieval strengthen retention without narrowing the content that counts as learnable?
  • What differences emerge between massed, spaced, and interleaved practice when the target outcome is proof or transfer rather than routine performance?
  • How do francophone didactic perspectives reframe the design problem usually described as spacing?
  • What institutional conditions are necessary for spacing to move from individual study advice to course-level design?

Use this as a closing field note

The deepest unfinished business in this literature is not whether spacing “works.” It is whether mathematics education can design revisiting over time in ways that preserve structure, depth, and epistemic seriousness.
